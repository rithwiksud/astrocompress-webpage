AstroCompress: A Benchmark Dataset for Multi-Purpose Compression of Astronomical Imagery

Tuan Truong*, Rithwik Sudharsan*, Yibo Yang, Peter Xiangyuan Ma, Ruihan Yang, Stephan Mandt, Joshua S. Bloom
UC Irvine, UC Berkeley, LBNL
Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2025
*Indicates Equal Contribution
Paper Supplementary Dataset arXiv

In the last two decades, there has been little to no advancement in compression algorithms for astronomical data. Due to weak downlink bandwidth, space telescopes must discard, alter, or simply not collect vast amounts of potential data before transmission to Earth. Improving lossless compression by even a few percentage points on these billion-dollar instruments can create enormous scientific value. AstroCompress benchmarks state-of-the-art methods and works toward delivering these critical improvements to the astronomy community.

Abstract

The site conditions that make astronomical observatories in space and on the ground so desirable—cold and dark—demand a physical remoteness that leads to limited data transmission capabilities. Such transmission limitations directly bottleneck the amount of data acquired and in an era of costly modern observatories, any improvements in lossless data compression has the potential scale to billions of dollars worth of additional science that can be accomplished on the same instrument. Traditional lossless methods for compressing astrophysical data are manually designed. Neural data compression, on the other hand, holds the promise of learning compression algorithms end-to-end from data and outperforming classical techniques by leveraging the unique spatial, temporal, and wavelength structures of astronomical images.

This paper introduces AstroCompress (https://huggingface.co/AnonAstroData): a neural compression challenge for astrophysics data, featuring four new datasets (and one legacy dataset) with 16-bit unsigned integer imaging data in various modes: space-based, ground-based, multi-wavelength, and time-series imaging. We provide code to easily access the data and benchmark seven lossless compression methods (three neural and four non-neural, including all practical state-of-the-art algorithms). Our results on lossless compression indicate that lossless neural compression techniques can enhance data collection at observatories, and provide guidance on the adoption of neural compression in scientific applications. Though the scope of this paper is restricted to lossless compression, we also comment on the potential exploration of lossy compression methods in future studies.

Main Contributions

  • A large (~320 GB), novel dataset captures a broad range of real astrophysical imaging data, carefully separated into train and test sets, with easy access via HuggingFace datasets.
  • An extensive comparison of lossless classical and neural compression methods on this data, the first publication to our knowledge where neural compression has been systematically studied on astronomical imaging.
  • Various qualitative analyses that further our understanding of the bit allocation in astronomical images and that inform potential exploration to future lossy compression codec designs.

AstroCompress Datasets

Our dataset consists of 5 distinct datasets, spanning a variety of observing conditions from space and from Earth, types of detector technology, and large dynamic ranges.

GBI-16-2D (Keck)

This is a diverse, 2D optical imaging dataset from the ground-based W. M. Keck Observatory. It contains 137 images of size either 2248 × 2048 or 3768 × 2520 pix², obtained in a variety of observing conditions, filters, and across exposure times from seconds to >10 min.

SBI-16-2D (Hubble)

This dataset is derived from the Hubble Space Telescope (HST) Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS) observations in the F606W (~red) filter. It contains 4282 images of size 4144 × 2068 pix². A major challenge (and opportunity for compression) in these raw space-based images are the preponderance of random cosmic ray-affected pixels and charge transfer inefficiencies causing vertical stripping.

SBI-16-3D (JWST)

This dataset comes from the NIRCAM instrument onboard the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), taken with the F200W (infrared) filter. It contains 1273 3D cubes of T× 2048 × 2048, for time steps T, typically ranging as 5 ⪅ T ⪅ 10. The instrument repeatedly measures the cumulative optimal charge across time, allowing for residual coding.

GBI-16-4D (SDSS)

This ground-based dataset is assembled from the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS). We assembled 500 four-dimensional cube representations of different 800×800 pix² portions of the sky, each one observed from t = 1 up to T ≈ 90 times in F=5 filters (u, g, r, i, z), each cube having shape T × F × 800 × 800.

GBI-16-2D-Legacy

This small ground-based dataset obtained on multiple CCDs across many different telescopes is reproduced from the public corpus released by Maireles-González et al. (2023) and assembled in HuggingFace dataset format.

Compression Results

We benchmark seven lossless compression methods (three neural and four non-neural) on our datasets:

Neural Methods:

  • Integer Discrete Flows (IDF): A flow-based model extending normalizing flows for lossless compression
  • L3C: A VAE-based lossless image compression method utilizing a two-part coding scheme
  • PixelCNN++: An autoregressive model using masked convolutions

Classical Methods:

  • JPEG-XL: Recent image codec with standard and maximum effort modes
  • JPEG-LS: Lossless JPEG standard
  • JPEG-2000: Wavelet-based compression standard
  • RICE: Classic compression method commonly used in astronomy

Key findings:

  • PixelCNN++ frequently achieves the best performance, often closely matching JPEG-XL (max) with less than a 4% difference
  • IDF demonstrates competitive performance on many datasets, despite being non-autoregressive
  • Non-neural codecs show superior performance boosts on our correlated-frame data, indicating a need for further improvement in neural compression techniques to better extract cross-wavelength or cross-timestep information

Effect of Noise on Compression

There is an inverse relationship between compression ratios and exposure time, which is one of the key variables in determining the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) in an image. Longer exposure times result in an increased number of noise bits, reducing compressibility.

Future Directions

  • Lossy compression exploration: Astronomy data, where most pixels are dominated by noise, is particularly promising for lossy compression approaches that could selectively discard non-essential data.
  • Better time-series compression: Exploiting correlations across time for frames collected back-to-back could yield significantly higher compression ratios.
  • Encode-decode time tradeoffs: Astronomy prioritizes compression performance and manageable encoding times, with slow decoding on the ground being acceptable. Future work could explore trading decoding speed for higher compression ratios.
  • Data specificity vs. generality exploration: Finding the right balance between specialized compression for specific instruments versus more general approaches.

Dataset Access

All datasets are released under the AstroCompress project as HuggingFace datasets and may be accessed as numpy nd-arrays in Python:

import numpy as np
from datasets import load_dataset

dataset = load_dataset(f"AstroCompress/{name}", config, split=split, \
                      streaming=True, trust_remote_code=True)
ds = dataset.with_format("np", columns=["image"], dtype=np.uint16)
ds[0]["image"].shape  # --> (tuple with shape of the numpy array)
